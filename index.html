Black Holes

Overview

Black holes are bodies that have such intense gravitational fields that both matter and light cannot escape it. The higher the density of the black hole, the stronger the gravitational field on its surface will be. For a celestial object to become a black hole, a density would have to be reached such that the subatomic particles of the object cannot support their own weight, and they collapse

Spacetime and the Event Horizon

Spacetime

Essentially, general relativity explains that space is curved, which is why gravity exists. It furthermore states that time and space are not separate, but they together form spacetime, which is a geometry formed from space and time together. Therefore the straight paths in this curved spacetime are geodesics, and the shortest path between two points is taken by a beam of light A geodesic will be more curved if the gravitational field is stronger in that region. So the geodesics in a black hole are so intensely curved that photons, which are packets of light, are not able to escape

Event Horizon

Since there are no geodesics which allow for straight lines in a black hole’s radius, a point in a black hole which is connected with a point anywhere else will essentially have no dimension. Black holes have an event horizon, which is their spherical surface that blocks off the events taking place inside the black hole to anything happening outside of it. Therefore, the event horizon is the boundary on a black hole where information cannot be observed by anyone, therefore has no effect on that observer. It can also be described as the area around the gravitational field of a black hole. The only things that can be observed of a black hole from spacetime is its mass, rate of spin, and net charge

History

Laplace was a French physicist who made significant advancements on the theory behind black holes before black holes was even a term. In his publishment called Exposition du Systeme du Monde (1796), Laplace use Michell’s theory to make more advancements to it. Using Newton’s corpuscular theory, which states that light emits ‘minute particles’ which are called corpuscles, that there should be stars which do not let any light emit out from it. Laplace did include calculations of escape velocity, but light not being able to escape a gravitational field was not numerically reasonable to showcase during this era.

Applications in Space

Chandra X-Ray Observatory

The Chandra X-Ray Observatory pinpointed a supermassive black hole that ripped through a star, and is leveraging the wreckage of that star to destroy another one. This supermassive black hole destroyed the star, and it created a ring of debris around the black hole. This ring of debris is in the orbital plane of the other star, which is colliding with it, releasing X-rays which the Chandra X-Ray Observatory is detecting

Applications in Physics

Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics triumphs over other theories in painting the picture of the physical universe at a micro-level, and yes, it triumphs general relativity in that aspect as well! As of right now, no one has been able to take both general relativity and quantum mechanics to better our understanding of the universe. But, in 1974, Stephen Hawkings stated that a black hole should theoretically radiate energy, according to quantum principles. This was called the Hawking radiation, and it cannot possibly be done by the protons which leave a black hole’s surface. This statement from Hawkings showed that black holes could potentially be the link between general relativity and quantum mechanics.

Thermodynamics

While black holes do “bend” the laws of physics, no pun intended, they should still be dutiful to them. One of these laws is the second law of thermodynamics, which states that during any process, the universe tends towards disorder, or entropy. So the higher something’s entropy is, the more disordered it is, in simple terms. When black holes absorb huge celestial masses, the mass's entropy decreases. This may not seem intuitive at first, since the mass is getting absorbed, but what makes this make sense is that the black hole in turn gains more entropy, since now the mass is going into the black hole. This idea is the basis behind the holographic principle, and gives us a better understanding of thermodynamics in the universe

Gravitational Waves

Gravitational waves were only really a theory until 2015. Basically, spacetime, as explained in its section, is distorted by objects which accelerate. This causes waves to propagate at light speed: gravitational waves. This was theorized in 1916, but in 2015, the LIGO and Virgo detected gravitational waves being created from two black holes merging together. Such a discovery led to the scientists working on this to win the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics.

Types of Obstacles

Financial

  • As of right now, the vast majority of space missions dedicated to black hole observations are conducted by NASA. However, the missions which NASA carries out may take a large dip. In 2024, Congress began to cut money from NASA’s budget
  • In NASA’s final budget for 2024, they had more than $2 billion worth in space initiatives and research projects cut off from the government, which included Black Hole space initiatives
  • The European Space Agency’s budget for 2025 has taken a 1.4% decrease from 2024, despite the great amount of missions they have for black hole research.
  • This is not the first time where financial obstacles have hindered black hole research. In 2012, the Gravity and Extreme Magnetism Small Explorer mission, which was planned to observe X-ray emissions from black holes, was abruptly cancelled due to the budget going roughly $50 million over their capacity.
  • Furthermore, in this past year, the Chandra X-ray observatory, which has made groundbreaking contributions to black hole research as shown above, has had its funding cut by nearly 40%, and there are plans to have more funding cuts for the observatory.

Scientific / Technological

  • The laws of the universe seem to bend, quite literally, when it comes to black holes. A scientific obstacle that arises when studying black holes is when studying them in relation to Einsteinian cubic gravity, which is an extension of general relativity. Even though black holes have unique and cool properties, when Einstein’s equation is applied to them, it cannot be solved numerically. This showcases the difficulty behind mathematically modelling black holes
  • Black holes do not emit any light, therefore the methods used to detect black holes relies on observing the matter around it instead
  • The singularity of a black hole, which is basically the center of it, breaks the laws of quantum theory and general relativity.
  • The data as of right now collected by telescopes such as the EHT has to be physically transferred from locations like the South Pole which can take nearly a year to accomplish
